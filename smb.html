ASK: What do you think the difference between a SBUAA and an MTU is?
Answer: An SBUAA may be a business that has a tenant relationship but is the only business in the building. For instance, a call center that leases the building that it's in and there are no other businesses in the building. Just like an MTU the SBUAA is technically not eligible until the access agreement is signed.

SBUAA's, sMTU's, and MTU's are all business that require an AA or Access Agreement before signing up. Once access arrangements (AAs) are signed, multi tenant business customers (MTUs and sMTUs) can sign up at any time, even after sign up closes. sMTUs and MTUs can sign up without a signed Access Agreement but they will still need the AA signed before install happens.

How is an SMB Install different than a Residential Install?

The small business installation process is similar to an MDU or in-home installation process. However, there's a few key differences...

  • Installation appointment duration is ~2 hours.
  • Gig and 2 Gig services only. Option to add 1, 5, or 13 Static IPs.
  • Only uses 2 Gig hardware (Pluggable ONT and Multi-Gig RG).
  • (Legacy Gig and SMB 250 plans utilize v.2 FJ/ONT and NB)
  • They are not offered the one terabyte of Google Drive Storage.
  • Site survey is performed and responses are provided in installation ticket.
  • Customers who sign up for 5 or 13 Static IPs Must Use Their Own Router.
  • Different cable running/wall-fishing considerations in a commercial building environment.
  • The installer is not responsible for configuring service to the customer's third-party business equipment (servers, VOIPs, wireless APIs, POS).
  • The consult and demo is slightly adapted to the small business customer.

Small Business Installer Troubleshooting

Troubleshooting Gig with No Static IP(s)

  • Follow ALL existing in-home installation troubleshooting steps.
  • SAY: Enable Bridge Mode on RG if customer is using 3rd Party router/firewall.

Troubleshooting Gig with 1 Static IP

  • Follow ALL existing in-home installation troubleshooting steps.
  • Additionally: If the customer is troubleshooting issues with a device which requires a static IP, advise them to see if the DHCP is set to provision static IP addresses (not manually).
  • Advise them that their static IP may not be assigned yet.
  • Enable Bridge-Mode on RG if customer is using 3rd Party router/firewall. (No other devices may be plugged into the RG while in Bridge-mode).

Troubleshooting Gig with 5 or 13 Static IPs

  • Follow all existing SMB installation troubleshooting steps.
  • Additionally:
    • Ensure the customer's 3rd party router is Gig-certified and DHCP is set to provision static IP addresses (not manually).
    • Advise the customer that their static IP may not have been assigned yet.
    • Plug in CAT5e from the MultiGig port to your Chromebook and try to reach the internet.
    • No other devices can be plugged into the RG while in Bridge Mode (MultiGig Port ONLY).
    • Escalate as necessary.

SMB Networking & Static IP Review

Some SMB Services Require Static IPs

[Image Placeholder: Icons for Web/Email/FTP, Applications, Audio/Video Services]

Examples:

  • Hosts, Serves, Connects a Business Website or Email Server, VPN, etc.
  • POS/Point of Sale Systems. Services that require a dedicated IP or IP with ports.
  • Hosted PBX telephone system, VOIP, and video conferencing services.
Most Fiber customers do not need any static IP addresses. However, you might need static IPs if one or more of these statements applies to you:
  • Maintain your own web server or other Internet server, such as an email server, that requires users to connect from the Internet.
  • Have internal devices or services that must connect to your network via IP address (for example, 74.125.224.72) rather than by name (for example, example.com).
  • Use a VPN or other service to remotely connect to computers in your business.

In many cases you can host a server on an Internet connection without static IPs by using Dynamic DNS. With Dynamic DNS, when your IP address changes, your DNS entry is automatically updated with your new IP address. The Google Fiber Network Box includes support for Dynamic DNS. You can set up Dynamic DNS by following these instructions.

If you are not sure whether you need static IPs, consult with your IT expert. Note that you can add or remove static IPs from your account, even after your service starts.

How to set up a "1 static IP customer" in My Fiber

[Image Placeholder: My Fiber Network Settings page]

Navigate to Network Settings (fiber.google.com). With the RG and 1 static IP, you should use the built-in port forwarding feature.

  • Go to Advanced settings.
  • Click Ports tab.
  • Toggle uPnP to ON to allow uPnP devices on your network to set up Port Forwarding rules for themselves.
  • After setting up Port Forwarding, those devices appear in the list, along with the ports, protocol, and service to which each is mapped.
  • If you disable uPnP, the Residential Gateway stops forwarding incoming requests to ports for which UPnP port forwarding rules are in effect.

Third-Party Routers: Bridge Mode MUST be Enabled on RG

All SMB Customers may choose to connect their own 3rd party router directly to the Multi-Gig port on the RG in Bridge-Mode. SMB Customers with 5 or 13 Static IP Addresses are required to do so.

  • Connect to Multi-Gig Ethernet WAN port on RG.
  • Set 3rd Party Router to DHCP and not manually add static IPs on its interface.
  • LAN to WAN throughput: at least 800 Mbps.
  • Forwarding and Network Address Translation (NAT) of private LAN addresses to public WAN addresses.

Customer steps to enable Bridge Mode:

  1. Customer logs into their Fiber account.
  2. Click on Network tab, then click on RG.
  3. Navigate to Admin tab.
  4. Place a check in the "Enable bridge mode" checkbox, then click APPLY.
  5. Once the RG reboots and comes online (the LED on RG is solid blue) you can verify Network Setting is off and cannot be toggled ON.

Single Static IP Configuration Details

[Image Placeholder: Static IPv4 Configuration Information box]

When reading a Single Static IP Configuration, you need to know a few things.

  • The first three octets are considered the Network Host.
  • The Last Octet is the Host ID.
  • The WAN (via DHCP) is the customer's single Static IP Address.
  • This information will be available through CSA, an email sent directly to the customer, and through the customer's MyFiber page.
[Image Placeholder: Diagram of Single Static IP Setup with NIU, RG in Bridge Mode, and 3rd Party Firewall]

Setup Notes:

  • Make sure the router connected to the MultiGig Port (with Bridge Mode enabled) is set to DHCP. The customer cannot manually add the static IP.
  • The Static IP: 23.228.143.200 is allocated to the RG via DHCP from the Google Fiber network.
  • On the Network tab, The RG has to be configured to port forward the appropriate ports that the server is hosting applications for. E.g. a web server would require an entry to allow incoming connections on port 80 to be forwarded to 192.168.1.100.

Multiple Static IP Configuration Details

[Image Placeholder: Multiple Static IPv4 Configuration Information box with CIDR]

When reading Multiple Static IP Configuration, there are a few differences.

  • In addition to the Network Host and Host ID you also have the CIDR.
  • The Host ID +1 will give you the Static LAN/Default Gateway.
  • Each sequential number after, will be your Public Static IP Addresses.
  • The Forward Slash is the CIDR (Classless Inter-Domain Routing) which tells you how many usable Static IPs the customer will have.
    • /29 will always have 5 usable Static IPs.
    • /28 will always have 13 usable Static IPs.
    • Refer to the Static IP Calculator if you have any question on what the customer's IPs should be.
[Image Placeholder: Diagram of Multiple Static IP Setup with NIU, RG in Bridge Mode, and 3rd Party Firewall with multiple web servers]

Setup Notes:

  • If you have a multi-layer router or firewall and router configuration, the customer must set up a static route to the first device.
  • Router connected to the MultiGig Port (with Bridge Mode enabled) is set to DHCP and that one of its ports is set as the gateway.
  • Make sure the remaining routers have static routes pointing to the gateway.
  • The Static IP: 23.288.168.65 is persistently allocated to the RG via DHCP from the Google Fiber network and 216.21.162.216/29 is routed from our network to 23.288.168.65.

  • Fine caulking new penetrations.
  • Wall fishing in a commercial environment (10' + ceiling heights).

  • Running through red iron, D-Rings for vertical runs, J-hooks, etc...
  • Always follow provided specs. If not possible, contact your Master Tech or Supervisor.
  • Installing conduit & cable raceways upon customer request.

  • Use existing jet line (wire pull) for new wire pulls.
  • Always replace with a chaser (follow up).
  • Protecting the fiber while pulling it with jet line.

Firestop Procedures

Ensuring your installation meets proper safety codes.

What is a Firestop?

  • SAY: A firestop is a fire protection system made of various components used to seal openings and joints in fire-resistance rated wall and/or assemblies. Firestops are designed to restore the fire-resistance ratings of wall and/or floor assemblies by impeding the spread of fire.
  • This is done by filling the openings with fire-resistant materials. Unprotected openings in fire separations cancel out the fire-resistance ratings of the fire separations.
  • This allows the spread of fire, usually past the limits of the fire safety plan of a building.
[**Play Video** - Image Placeholder: Firestop Devices Video Frame]

Small Business: Firestop Materials

[Image Placeholder: Firestop Putty and Firestop Grommets]
  • Firestop Putty: Used around and inside firewall penetrations. (Tear, Knead, Place, Fill).
    1. Tear off an appropriate amount of putty.
    2. Knead and shape the putty as desired.
    3. Place the putty completely around your passthrough device (conduit, sleeve, etc...).
    4. When you are finished installing your cables, fill both ends of the conduit with firestop putty as well. If there was an existing firestop pillow, putty or other material, don't forget to put it back.
  • Firestop Grommets: Devices that will be used for all new firewall penetrations. (Drill, Run, Snap, Insert).
    1. Drill a one inch hole through the firewall.
    2. Run your cable through the new penetration.
    3. Snap both pieces of the grommet together around your cable.
    4. Insert the grommet into the hole. You will do this on both sides of the wall.

Other Firestop Materials and Devices

[Image Placeholder: Firestop Pillows, EZ Path Devices, Firestop Plugs]
  • There are many other types of firestop systems and materials you will come across.
  • You will see a lot of approved systems, but that doesn't mean they are installed correctly.
  • Do not use an existing firestop pathway if it unsafe.
  • Whenever possible, we want to utilize any existing firestop penetrations that are available, as long as they will meet fire code requirements.

Common Firestop Violations

[Image Placeholder: Common Firestop Violations - Wrong vs. Right]

Let's explore some common firestop violations:

  • Conduit penetration is not sealed properly
  • Conduits are missing internal firestop
  • Expanding foam is not a firestop material

Firecode and Liability

Whether you use an existing penetration or you create a new one, you must comply with fire and safety codes.

It is very important that we restore the firestop rating to any openings or firestop systems we use. That means, if you have to remove firestop material from a conduit to run your cable through, you need to put it back when you are finished.

If you are the last man out, it is your responsibility to maintain the Fire Rating of the structure being used. Failure to do so can result in fines, and possibly even negligence charges.

Comm Room Security & Privacy

When you are given access to a comm room, YOU become fully responsible for any damages or theft that may occur from negligence. That means, if you leave the comm room unattended and someone gains access to it, you could be liable. There is no excuse for leaving a comm room door unattended.

There will be times when you need to leave the comm room:

  • You need to use the bathroom.
  • You left tools or material in your van.
  • You have had cell phone reception.
  • You need to talk with the customer.

These are all valid reasons for leaving the comm room. However, we still need to follow security protocols.

Rules of Engagement

  • Secure the Room: If you leave the comm room, it is usually best to lock and close the door. Make sure someone is available to let you back in. Do not allow access to the comm room to any personnel. Always make sure the comm room is locked and secured and communicate that position with your point of contact before leaving the job site.
  • Do Not Share Sensitive Information: Comm rooms usually contain a lot of sensitive information that should never be shared with anyone other than the designated point of contact. You will often see documents in a comm room and in various places throughout the business. It is important that you never read any of these documents! They could contain very sensitive information, such as: financial data, employee records, etc... Never take photographs while in the business unless you have a valid reason to do so. You will need to obtain permission from your point of contact and notify your master technician or supervisor.
  • Never Unplug ANY Devices: Unplugging even just one cable could be catastrophic to the network and cause thousands of dollars in lost revenue. Similarly, plugging into a live circuit could have undesirable consequences. You could bring a device online and expose it to potential online dangers. It could also be perceived that you are attempting to steal company data. If anything has to be unplugged or plugged in, always let the customer do this.

Safety in Comm Rooms

  • Loose Wiring: The number one hazard in comm rooms is loose cables and wiring. Some comm rooms are very disorganized and hard to move around in. Snagging loose cables, causing them to break or unplug, is a very common problem.
  • Spatial Awareness: Tool pouches and ladders are the usual culprits, but carelessness is usually involved. Always scout out the comm room and identify any potential hazards before jumping in.
  • Proper Ladder Usage:
    • Do NOT lean Step Ladders on the Customer's Equipment, Server Racks, or Walls.
    • Do NOT climb on the Customer's Equipment, Server Racks, or Furniture.
    • If you cannot set up your ladder properly, politely ask the customer to move objects out of the way for a clear space to work. If this is not possible, notify your master technician or supervisor of the situation.
