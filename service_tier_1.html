Repair Technician Study Guide

This guide provides a comprehensive overview of the Repair Technician role, covering the Services & Expectations and the Understanding of the Repair Process. It is broken down into three main sections: an introduction to repair visits covering scope and diagnostics, a deep dive into advanced networking fundamentals, and a review of best effort practices for customer education and device troubleshooting.

Section 1: Introduction to Service Visits

This section covers the foundational knowledge for a service visit, including scope of work, customer interaction, expected speeds, and structured processes for troubleshooting.

Scope of Work (Slide 4, 17)

The Scope of Work defines how far we go during a service visit. It's in place so we are all on the same page and the customer receives consistent experience regardless of the technician.

Core Principles:

  • Follow established Protocols & Procedures.
  • Understand company Liability to avoid overstepping boundaries.
  • When in Doubt: Always reach out to your Lead Tech or Supervisor. Going outside the scope breaks the standard of work we all follow.

What is Best Effort? (Slide 8)

Best Effort means following the installation and troubleshooting steps with the equipment we are assisting with. This includes:

  • Google Fiber CPE
  • Customer Owned Equipment (COE)
  • Customer Education

Effectively managing customer frustration is a critical first step. Remember, they are upset with the situation, not necessarily with you.

  1. Listen without emotion: Give the customer your full attention and do not interrupt. When they are finished, let them know you were listening by calmly summarizing what they said.
  2. Let the customer know you are listening: Use active listening and acknowledge their points.
  3. Offer a Reply: Apologize, empathize, and maintain a calm and caring tone.
  4. Take Responsibility: Take responsibility for the situation, even if you aren't at fault. DO NOT pass the buck, offer excuses, or blame a previous technician.
  5. Resolve or Repair as Quickly as Possible: Be prepared with a plan of action and implement it soon.
  6. Follow Up: Before leaving, tell the customer what problems you found, what you did to fix them, and any preventative measures taken.

Factors Affecting Speed:

  • Out-of-date hardware: The type of hardware you use, age of the device, operating system, web browser, network interface card (NIC), available RAM, Hard Drive Space, and other applications all affect speed.
  • Network Path: Slow connections between Google Fiber's network and the websites you visit can reduce speed due to heavy traffic or suboptimal routing.
  • VPNs (Virtual Private Networks): If the VPN's server isn't capable of forwarding traffic at 1 gigabit, your speed may be reduced to the speed the VPN server is capable of.
  • Latency: The measurement of how long it takes to transmit or receive packets. Affected by distance and the number of network packets to travel over.
  • Packet loss: Occurs when packets fail to arrive. Caused by network congestion, faulty hardware, poor device performance, or software bugs.

A systematic method for problem-solving that ensures time is spent assessing the situation and determining what needs to be changed.

  1. Identify the Problem:
    • Look at Previous Ticket Notes.
    • Ask the Customer Relevant Questions.
    • Examine the Physical Layer.
  2. Establish a Theory of Probable Cause:
    • Make a List of Probable Causes using your knowledge.
    • Prioritize the List and Question the Obvious.
  3. Test Potential Solutions:
    • Create your theory from the Most Probable Cause.
    • If the Theory is Confirmed, Move On.
    • If the Theory is NOT Confirmed, go back and establish a new theory or escalate.
  4. Act on Potential Solutions:
    • Plug-in Power Supply, Clean the Fiber, Factory/Circuit Reset, Escalate.
  5. Test, Analyze & Prevent:
    • Verify full system functionality is restored.
    • Implement preventative measures so the problem does not occur again.
    • If not fully functional, go back to step one.
  6. Evaluate and Report the Outcome:
    • Document and Notate Findings, Actions Taken, and Outcomes.
    • Educate the Customer on what you found and what you changed.
    Notate, Notate, Notate. Clear and detailed notes are crucial.

Section 2: Advanced Networking

This section explores technical networking topics including Wi-Fi fundamentals, IP addressing, and essential network protocols. This contains pertinent information for daily jobs as service technicians.

How Wi-Fi Works (Slide 35)

Simply put, Wi-Fi transmits radio waves. It operates on two different ISM (Industrial, Scientific, and Medical) bands, 2.4GHz and 5GHz networks. Radio waves can be impacted by numerous factors, which can affect the overall signal quality, speed, and range.

2.4 GHz vs 5 GHz Networks (Slide 37)

Channel Bonding & Interference (Slides 39-41)

Think of it like an information highway. A single lane (20 MHz) causes traffic congestion. Channel Bonding opens up more lanes (40, 80, 160 MHz), allowing more information to pass with less resistance.

  • 802.11n allows up to 40 MHz channel bonding.
  • 802.11ac allows up to 80 MHz channel bonding.

The more overlapping networks you have, the greater the chance of network interference. Multi-Channel Bonded signals give your devices more options for a clear path to pass information.

Potential Sources of Wi-Fi Interference (Slide 42)

  • Structural: Dense Walls, Metal & Concrete, Mirrors
  • Appliances: TVs, Refrigerators, Microwaves
  • Other Electronics: Baby Monitors, Bluetooth Devices, Cordless Phones
Importance of Device Placement: The best place for a router is in a central, open location. A cabinet, behind a TV, or in the basement are not optimal locations for signal propagation.

TCP/IP vs OSI Model (Slide 46)

We primarily deal with the lower layers:

  • Layer 3 (Network Layer): This would be your routers. It handles all of your routing, IP addresses, etc. Also handles packets of data.
  • Layer 2 (Data Link Layer): This would be your switches. It only concerns itself with MAC addresses and handles frames of data.
  • Layer 1 (Physical Layer): This would be all your cables, network cards, etc. It handles raw bits of data.

MAC vs IP Address (Slides 47-48)

  • MAC Address: A unique, hard-coded 12-digit hexadecimal identifier for a network interface (Ethernet, Wi-Fi). It's like a device's fingerprint, operating at Layer 2.
  • IP Address: A unique address for a device on a network, assigned for communication. It's like a house's mailing address, operating at Layer 3.

IP Types (Static, Dynamic, Public, Private) (Slides 50-51)

  • Static IP: Does not change. Only supported on SMB.
  • Dynamic IP: Changes over time. Supported on Residential and SMB.
  • Public (External) IP: Assigned by your ISP. Identifies your network to the internet.
  • Private (Local) IP: Assigned by your router. Identifies devices within your home network.

DHCP, APIPA, DNS (Slides 52-55)

  • DHCP (Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol): Automatically assigns private IP addresses to devices on a network.
  • APIPA (Automatic Private IP Addressing): A fallback that self-assigns an IP if DHCP fails. An IP address starting with 169.254.x.x indicates a problem with DHCP routing.
  • DNS (Domain Name System): A phone book for the internet. Translates domain names (e.g., google.com) to IP addresses.
    Troubleshooting DNS on Chromebook: Open Crosh (CTRL+ALT+T). Type `ping [ip address]` or `ping [domain name]`. If pinging the IP works but the name fails, the issue is DNS.
  • Dynamic DNS: Automatically updates a server in the DNS system. Useful for remote access, Active Directory, or changing device locations. Can be used in lieu of a static IP for residential customers.

NAT, Subnets, Port Forwarding (Slides 56-58)

  • NAT (Network Address Translation): Allows a router to use a single public IP to represent a whole private network.
    Double NAT: Caused by two unbridged routers both performing NAT. Devices on different routers can't communicate, blocking access to game servers, printers, etc.
  • Subnet/Subnet Mask: A subnet is a logical subdivision of a network. A subnet mask is an identifier for routing traffic within that subnet.
  • Port Forwarding: Opens ports in a firewall to allow outside traffic to reach a specific device for services like IP cameras or game servers.
    Forwarding will break if the device's IP address is not static or reserved.
  • UPnP (Universal Plug and Play): Allows for automatic port forwarding. It's on by default but can be unreliable and a security risk.
  • DMZ (Demilitarized Zone): Places a device outside the firewall, allowing ANY device to reach it.
    NEVER place or suggest a customer place their devices in the DMZ. It's a huge liability.

Section 3: Best Effort Practices & Device Scope

This section details the scope of support for various third-party devices and services. The core principle is "You own it once you touch it," meaning we must be careful and knowledgeable when assisting with customer-owned equipment.

What we DO support:

  • Network printing ONLY. We can help connect a network-capable printer to the Wi-Fi.
  • Maximum of one printer per household.

What we DO NOT support:

  • Scanner, direct printing, or other setups.
  • Driver or software installation.
  • File sharing setups.
  • Local Printing (e.g., USB): We do not set up local printing.
Every printer is different. Use Google as a resource. Make sure the customer knows all information and settings may be reconfigured. Once you touch it, you own it.

Bridging Routers (Slides 71-72)

We can "bridge" routers to act as an access point and assist in setting up a mesh network. Most customers only need one router bridged. Bridging drops a router from a Layer 3 device (router) to a Layer 2 device (switch with Wi-Fi).

Double NAT: Occurs when two routers are not bridged, preventing communication between devices on each network and blocking access to game servers, FTP, printers, etc.

How to Bridge a Router (General Steps - Slide 73):

  1. Always get customer permission before doing this.
  2. Factory Reset the Router: Clears custom settings.
  3. Disable all internet on the bridging computer (including mobile data).
  4. Plug your computer into one of the router's LAN ports.
  5. Login to the router (e.g., admin/admin, admin/password).
  6. Configure the Wi-Fi and Enable Bridge Mode (or AP Mode).
  7. Plug an internet source (NB or G-Wifi Point) into the router's LAN port.
  8. Verify full functionality (Wi-Fi bands and Web Access/DNS).

Apple Airport Utility (Slide 74)

Apple routers are much easier to set up. Connect to the same network and the utility will find the devices.

  • On the Primary Base Station: go to Network Tab: Router Mode: Off (Bridge Mode)
  • On Time Capsule: go to Disk Tab: Enable File Sharing. Then run the Time Machine program on their MAC to verify it's functioning.
We do not configure or adjust backups or settings!
For all unsupported devices, the general rule is: you can provide customer education, but you are not allowed to touch or configure the device. You should direct the customer to the KB port forwarding article for reference if applicable.
  • Home Theatre Systems: Keep it simple (soundbars, basic receivers). This is best effort. Only do what you are comfortable with.
  • VPN (Virtual Private Network): WE DO NOT SUPPORT VPNs.
  • Security Systems (CCTV): WE DO NOT SUPPORT SECURITY SYSTEMS. Do not touch or configure DVRs/NDRs or cameras.
  • VoIP: We DO connect VoIP systems to the internet via Ethernet. We DO NOT connect phone lines/jacks or configure the device.
  • IoT & Home Automation: WE DO NOT SUPPORT THESE DEVICES. If the problem is connection-related, verify Wi-Fi is working from your laptop. If it's a firewall/NAT issue, educate and direct to the KB article.

This can be a tricky area. We want to resolve issues without going too far.

What we CAN do:

  • Investigate and solve network related problems only.
  • Use DOS commands, reset the TCP/IP stack, configure basic settings, etc.
  • Install a web browser (preferably Chrome from an official site) to isolate browser and connection issues.

What we DO NOT do:

  • Install drivers or updates.
  • Uninstall software.
  • Troubleshoot 3rd party problems (e.g., email clients, tune-up devices).
