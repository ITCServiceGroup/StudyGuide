What is Best Effort? (Slide 8)

Best Effort means following the installation and troubleshooting steps with the equipment we are assisting with. This includes:

  • Google Fiber CPE
  • Customer Owned Equipment (COE)
  • Customer Education

Effectively managing customer frustration is a critical first step. Remember, they are upset with the situation, not necessarily with you.

  1. Listen without emotion: Give the customer your full attention and do not interrupt. When they are finished, let them know you were listening by calmly summarizing what they said.
  2. Let the customer know you are listening: Use active listening and acknowledge their points.
  3. Offer a Reply: Apologize, empathize, and maintain a calm and caring tone.
  4. Take Responsibility: Take responsibility for the situation, even if you aren't at fault. DO NOT pass the buck, offer excuses, or blame a previous technician.
  5. Resolve or Repair as Quickly as Possible: Be prepared with a plan of action and implement it soon.
  6. Follow Up: Before leaving, tell the customer what problems you found, what you did to fix them, and any preventative measures taken.

Factors That Can Slow Your Network Speed

  • Out-of-date hardware: The type of hardware you use, age of the device, operating system, web browser, network interface card (NIC), available RAM, Hard Drive Space, and other applications running on your device all affect upload and download speeds.
  • Slow connections between Google Fiber's network and the websites you visit: Once your communication leaves the Fiber network, we can’t ensure you’ll receive maximum speeds due to heavy traffic or substantial rerouting delays at any time.
  • VPNs (Virtual Private Networks): If the VPN’s server isn’t capable of forwarding traffic at 1 gigabit, your Internet speed may be reduced to the speed the VPN server is capable of.
  • Latency: Latency is the measurement of how long it takes to transmit or receive packets on a particular network, and is affected by how far packets need to travel, how many network packets need to travel over, and the quality of those networks.
  • Packet loss: Like latency, packet loss can have a number of different causes, including network congestion, faulty hardware, poor device performance or the presence of software bugs.

How Wi-Fi Works (Slide 35)

Simply put, Wi-Fi transmits radio waves. It operates on different ISM (Industrial, Scientific, and Medical) bands. Radio waves can be impacted by numerous factors, which can affect the overall signal quality, speed, and range.

Understanding the 6 GHz Band

The 6 GHz band is a new, expansive frequency (5.925 GHz to 7.125 GHz) made available for Wi-Fi 6E and Wi-Fi 7 devices. [2, 6] Its main advantage is that it is a "clean" band, free from interference from older, legacy Wi-Fi devices. [1, 2] This leads to more reliable, lower-latency connections, especially in dense environments. [3, 8] However, the shorter wavelength means it has the most difficulty passing through walls and solid objects, making it best for high-performance use in the same room as the router. [13]

Channel Bonding & Interference (Slides 39-41)

Think of it like an information highway. A single lane (20 MHz) causes traffic congestion. Channel Bonding opens up more lanes (40, 80, 160 MHz), allowing more information to pass with less resistance.

  • 802.11n allows up to 40 MHz channel bonding.
  • 802.11ac allows up to 80 MHz channel bonding.
  • Wi-Fi 6/6E can use up to 160 MHz channels. [14]
  • Wi-Fi 7 doubles that to 320 MHz channels in the 6 GHz band for massive throughput. [14, 17]

The more overlapping networks you have, the greater the chance of network interference. Multi-Channel Bonded signals give your devices more options for a clear path to pass information.

Potential Sources of Wi-Fi Interference (Slide 42)

  • Structural: Dense Walls, Metal & Concrete, Mirrors
  • Appliances: TVs, Refrigerators, Microwaves
  • Other Electronics: Baby Monitors, Bluetooth Devices, Cordless Phones

Wi-Fi Encryption (WPA)

Wi-Fi encryption is a security protocol for wireless networks that encrypts the data being transmitted, preventing unauthorized access. Using the strongest available encryption is crucial for protecting user privacy and data.

  • WPA (Wi-Fi Protected Access): An older protocol that replaced the original, insecure WEP standard. It is now considered outdated and vulnerable.
  • WPA2 (Wi-Fi Protected Access II): The long-time standard for security, using strong AES encryption. It is still widely used and secure for most purposes, but is being superseded by WPA3.
  • WPA3 (Wi-Fi Protected Access 3): The current and most secure standard. It provides more robust authentication and increased cryptographic strength, making it much harder for attackers to crack passwords and eavesdrop on traffic, even on public networks.
Encryption's Impact on Wi-Fi Speed

It is critical to understand that the choice of encryption protocol directly impacts the maximum possible speed of a Wi-Fi network.

  • WPA (with TKIP): The original WPA protocol uses an encryption method called TKIP. The Wi-Fi standard (802.11n and newer) specifies that if TKIP is used, the network speed cannot exceed 54 Mbps. This is a hard-coded limitation to ensure network stability, as TKIP was not designed for the high throughput of modern Wi-Fi.
  • WPA2 & WPA3 (with AES): To achieve speeds greater than 54 Mbps (such as the 144+ Mbps rates of Wi-Fi 4 and beyond), the network must be configured to use at least WPA2 with AES encryption. WPA3 also uses AES-based encryption and is required for the highest performance modes of Wi-Fi 6 and Wi-Fi 7.

Therefore, if a network is slow, checking for an outdated WPA/TKIP security setting can be a valid troubleshooting step, as it creates a significant performance bottleneck.

Best Practice: Always configure a customer's network to use the highest level of security supported by their router and devices, which is ideally WPA3. If WPA3 is not available, WPA2-AES is the minimum secure standard that should be used.
Importance of Device Placement: The best place for a router is in a central, open location. A cabinet, behind a TV, or in the basement are not optimal locations for signal propagation.

Understanding network models and addressing is fundamental to network troubleshooting, as it explains how data moves through networks and how devices communicate with each other using various addressing schemes at different network layers.

TCP/IP vs OSI Model (Slide 46)

OSI Model (7 Layers)

  • Layer 7 (Application): End-user applications like web browsers, email clients. HTTP, FTP, SMTP protocols operate here.
  • Layer 6 (Presentation): Data formatting, encryption, compression. Ensures data is readable by the receiving system.
  • Layer 5 (Session): Manages communication sessions between applications. Handles authentication and reconnection.
  • Layer 4 (Transport): End-to-end communication control. TCP ensures reliable delivery, UDP for faster, unreliable delivery.
  • Layer 3 (Network): Routes data between networks using IP addresses. Your routers operate here handling data packets.
  • Layer 2 (Data Link): Direct node-to-node delivery using MAC addresses. Switches operate here handling data frames.
  • Layer 1 (Physical): Raw bit transmission over physical medium. Includes cables, network cards, fiber optics.

TCP/IP Model (4 Layers)

  • Application Layer: Combines OSI layers 5-7. Handles all application protocols and user interfaces.
  • Transport Layer: Equivalent to OSI Layer 4. Uses TCP for reliable or UDP for fast delivery.
  • Internet Layer: Equivalent to OSI Layer 3. Handles IP addressing and routing between networks.
  • Network Access Layer: Combines OSI layers 1-2. Handles physical transmission and hardware addressing.

As repair technicians, we primarily work with the lower layers where physical and addressing issues often occur:

  • Network/Internet Layer: Troubleshooting routing issues, IP addressing problems, and network connectivity.
  • Data Link Layer: Resolving switch configurations, MAC address issues, and local network connectivity.
  • Physical Layer: Dealing with cabling issues, signal problems, and hardware malfunctions.

MAC vs IP Address (Slides 47-48)

  • MAC Address: A unique, hard-coded 12-digit hexadecimal identifier for a network interface (Ethernet, Wi-Fi). It's like a device's fingerprint, operating at Layer 2.
  • IP Address: A unique address for a device on a network, assigned for communication. It's like a house's mailing address, operating at Layer 3.

IP Types (Static, Dynamic, Public, Private) (Slides 50-51)

  • Static IP: Does not change. Only supported on SMB.
  • Dynamic IP: Changes over time. Supported on Residential and SMB.
  • Public (External) IP: Assigned by your ISP. Identifies your network to the internet.
  • Private (Local) IP: Assigned by your router. Identifies devices within your home network.

Core network protocols are the fundamental building blocks of network communication, providing essential services like address assignment (DHCP), name resolution (DNS), and network address translation (NAT). Understanding these protocols is crucial for diagnosing network issues and ensuring proper network functionality.

DHCP, APIPA, DNS (Slides 52-55)

  • DHCP (Dynamic Host Configuration Protocol): Automatically assigns private IP addresses to devices on a network.
  • APIPA (Automatic Private IP Addressing): A fallback that self-assigns an IP if DHCP fails. An IP address starting with 169.254.x.x indicates a problem with DHCP routing.
  • DNS (Domain Name System): A phone book for the internet. Translates domain names (e.g., google.com) to IP addresses.
    Troubleshooting DNS on Chromebook: Open Crosh (CTRL+ALT+T). Type `ping [ip address]` or `ping [domain name]`. If pinging the IP works but the name fails, the issue is DNS.
  • Dynamic DNS: Automatically updates a server in the DNS system. Useful for remote access, Active Directory, or changing device locations. Can be used in lieu of a static IP for residential customers.

NAT, Subnets, Port Forwarding (Slides 56-58)

  • NAT (Network Address Translation): Allows a router to use a single public IP to represent a whole private network.
    Double NAT: Caused by two unbridged routers both performing NAT. Devices on different routers can't communicate, blocking access to game servers, printers, etc.
  • Subnet/Subnet Mask: A subnet is a logical subdivision of a network. A subnet mask is an identifier for routing traffic within that subnet.
  • Port Forwarding: Opens ports in a firewall to allow outside traffic to reach a specific device for services like IP cameras or game servers.
    Forwarding will break if the device's IP address is not static or reserved.
  • UPnP (Universal Plug and Play): Allows for automatic port forwarding. It's on by default but can be unreliable and a security risk.
  • DMZ (Demilitarized Zone): Places a device outside the firewall, allowing ANY device to reach it.
    NEVER place or suggest a customer place their devices in the DMZ. It's a huge liability.

Network printer setup requires a careful balance between providing necessary connectivity support while staying within our service boundaries. Understanding what we do and don't support helps maintain consistent service delivery and clear customer expectations.

What we DO support:

Router bridging is a critical solution for extending network coverage while avoiding common networking issues like Double NAT. Understanding the bridging process and best practices ensures successful integration of customer-owned equipment.

Bridging Routers (Slides 71-72)

We can "bridge" routers to act as an access point and assist in setting up a mesh network. Most customers only need one router bridged. Bridging drops a router from a Layer 3 device (router) to a Layer 2 device (switch with Wi-Fi).

Double NAT: Occurs when two routers are not bridged, preventing communication between devices on each network and blocking access to game servers, FTP, printers, etc.

How to Bridge a Router (General Steps - Slide 73):

  1. Always get customer permission before doing this.
  2. Factory Reset the Router: Clears custom settings.
  3. Disable all internet on the bridging computer (including mobile data).
  4. Plug your computer into one of the router's LAN ports.
  5. Login to the router (e.g., admin/admin, admin/password).
  6. Configure the Wi-Fi and Enable Bridge Mode (or AP Mode).
  7. Plug an internet source (NB or G-Wifi Point) into the router's LAN port.
  8. Verify full functionality (Wi-Fi bands and Web Access/DNS).

Apple Airport Utility (Slide 74)

Apple routers are much easier to set up. Connect to the same network and the utility will find the devices.

  • On the Primary Base Station: go to Network Tab: Router Mode: Off (Bridge Mode)
  • On Time Capsule: go to Disk Tab: Enable File Sharing. Then run the Time Machine program on their MAC to verify it's functioning.
We do not configure or adjust backups or settings!

For all unsupported devices, the general rule is: you can provide customer education, but you are not allowed to touch or configure the device. You should direct the customer to the KB port forwarding article for reference if applicable.
  • Home Theatre Systems: Keep it simple (soundbars, basic receivers). This is best effort. Only do what you are comfortable with.
